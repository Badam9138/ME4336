| **Subject** | The Feasibility in Reducing the Cost of Campus Food |

**Background**
According to an online survey that was conducted between third-year UL students, 73% of respondents believe that prices for food on campus is too high. Students complained of a much higher markup compared to equivalent off-campus options. When comparing similar food items, UL’s offerings averaged a 13% higher cost in comparison to restaurants located outside the university. These figures are a clear indication of a need to reduce the prices of food on campus. This report outlines the apparent causes of this issue as well as recommendations to subsequently address it.

**Causes of High Prices**
•	Expensive suppliers/inflation: As of October 2025, the Irish general inflation rate has fallen from previous years albeit grocery price inflation has reached its highest level since December 2023. This contributes directly to the increase in campus food prices.

•	Low competition: Aramark, an American food service provider, manages and operates nine of UL’s restaurants – a majority, including:
o	  Cafe Allegro – University Concert Hall
o	  Eden Restaurant – Main Building Block D Level M
o	  Cafe Cube – Kemmy Business School Ground Level
o	  River Cafe – Millstream Complex
o	  Cafe Verde – Health Science Ground Level
o  	Chopped, Mexico Kitchen, Subway, Starbucks – Main Building
This monopoly doesn’t encourage pricing variety as it is a low-competition environment. This also forces other independently operated restaurants, such as The Paddocks restaurant and The Pavilion, to raise their prices in order to compensate. 

•	UL Rent/Concession Fees: These fees are included in the tenders signed between UL and the restaurants and are subsequently passed on to customers (UL students) via higher food prices. A major issue arises in in the fact that due to Aramark managing the majority of of restaurants – they can get a much more competitive rate compared to independent restaurants, further strengthening their monopoly and reducing the competition on campus.

**Recommended Changes**
There are several possible options for reducing the cost of food on campus. The best two we believe would have the best outcome would be:
•	Increase competition through local tendering – Encourage local and independent food providers to bid for spaces on campus, reducing reliance on Aramark and diversifying food options.
•	UL subsidises part of the cost - If the university allocated funds or student welfare resources to offset vendor cost, menu prices could be reduced without negatively affecting the profitability of campus restaurants.
Supporting Data
Both options are the most realistic and feasible as they are both relatively easy and quick to implement. Both options would also complement each other and make the food on campus cheaper and more affordable for students. NUIG has already implemented this type of system that combines multiple food suppliers with partial university subsides for student meals. The university currently works with 5 different food providers compared to UL mainly using Aramark which has prevented a monopoly and allowed for a wider range of price options. 
If you compare the price of a coffee from UL and NUIG the average price of UL is €4.20 compared to only €3.60 in NUIG. A difference of €0.60 per coffee represents a significant saving for students with some meals costing €1- 2 less compared to UL. These differences show how supplier diversity is encouraging competitive pricing, with the university subsidies further reducing the final burden on students.
Both measures are realistic and straightforward to implement and together would make campus food more affordable without compromising quality. The success of NUIG’s approach shows that if University of Limerick also adopts this approach it will effectively lower food prices and improve student satisfaction.

**Alternative Solutions**
Another solution that would certainly reduce the cost of food on campus but would be very difficult to implement is having some of the local supermarkets such as Lidl, Aldi, Supervalu and Spar, support students by accepting discounts on common food items that students would buy multiple times on a weekly basis. These discounts could apply to premade sandwiches, deli items certain drinks and maybe pasta for home cooked meals. 
The reason this is an alternative solution is that it would be quite difficult to implement as the shops themselves don’t want to be losing profits. The shops would have to get the potential profits back from somewhere else, whether that be by increasing the prices on non-essential but common items like vapes or cigarettes or by increasing the cost of everything but the discounted items by a small margin or even by getting a sponsor in. 
There are these few incentives to have the shop cut the prices on common food items, but they all are quite difficult to implement and thus this solution is probably not feasible to solve the problem of expensive food on or around the campus. The other solutions listed above are a lot easier to implement into our campus.
Call to Action
It’s clear that food on campus is too expensive, and the current setup isn’t working for students. By following our recommendations of bringing in more local food providers and having the university cover part of the costs, UL can make real, positive changes. These steps would make food prices more affordable, give students more choice, and take pressure off those already struggling with living costs of rent and other common expenses. We’re asking the university and student representatives to take this issue seriously and act quickly to make campus food more affordable. Making these changes would show that UL truly cares about its students and is willing to support them in a meaningful way.
